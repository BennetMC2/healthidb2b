# Market Synthesis For Product Reset

## What current industry research says

This is the external backdrop for the first-principles reset.

## 1. Life insurers have a relevance problem

Capgemini’s 2025 life insurance report says many consumers are only mildly interested in traditional life insurance products, that customer expectations have risen sharply, and that onboarding, servicing, and claims remain weak for most insurers.

Implication for HealthID:

- the product must create visible customer value, not just insurer-side efficiency
- the operating loop must feel modern and high-frequency, not annual and administrative

## 2. Legacy systems still block meaningful change

Capgemini also highlights outdated technology as a major barrier to improving customer experience and executing broader transformation.

Implication for HealthID:

- buyers will not want a heavyweight rip-and-replace story
- the product should feel like a new operating layer that can coexist with insurer core systems

## 3. Digital engagement is now a core business requirement

Deloitte’s 2025 Digital Insurance Maturity research says digitally enabled engagement is no longer optional and that leading insurers need integrated, front-to-back transformation rather than surface-level digitization.

Implication for HealthID:

- the product cannot be just a dashboard
- it must feel like an integrated operating capability

## 4. Hybrid digital + human models matter

Deloitte also emphasizes hybrid models that combine digital capability with human advice, support, or oversight.

Implication for HealthID:

- the right design is not self-serve only
- the product should support insurer teams, forward-deployed support, and eventually agent-assisted motions

## 5. Underwriting modernization is real and persistent

Swiss Re has highlighted the push toward data-driven underwriting and more dynamic use of lifestyle and health factors, while LIMRA has repeatedly shown that simplified underwriting and faster issuance materially improve consumer receptivity.

Implication for HealthID:

- underwriting support remains a strong adjacent use case
- it should stay visible even if claims reduction is the lead narrative

## 6. Claims operations are a major strategic pain point

Swiss Re’s current claims materials emphasize cycle time, claimant experience, manual burden, and operational complexity. Claims is not just a servicing problem; it is a brand and profitability problem.

Implication for HealthID:

- a product that influences claims outcomes or claims operations can matter strategically
- the future product should include a true decision layer, not just campaign analytics

## 7. Wellness remains promising but hard to prove

RGA’s 2022 and 2025 wellness materials make the tension explicit:

- prevention and wellness can create real upside
- engagement and behavioral change are possible
- measuring claims impact remains difficult
- many insurers still lack aligned metrics or strong execution

Implication for HealthID:

- claims reduction can be the lead story
- but the product must be disciplined about how it presents evidence

## 8. Customer expectations center on simplicity, speed, transparency, and value

LIMRA’s 2026 customer expectations work emphasizes simplicity, transparency, speed, and meaningful value beyond the policy itself.

Implication for HealthID:

- if the member proposition is weak, participation will break
- if the insurer workflow is cumbersome, adoption will break

## 9. AI matters where workflows are fragmented and judgment-heavy

OpenAI’s current guidance is clear that agents are best suited to multi-step workflows involving context, judgment, tools, and handoffs. Microsoft’s 2026 insurance post similarly points to agentic systems reshaping insurance where workflows are fragmented and operationally heavy.

Implication for HealthID:

- an agent is justified
- but only as a workflow-native strategy and decision-support layer

## Strategic takeaways

From these sources, the most defensible product conclusions are:

- build an insurer operating layer, not a wellness app
- lead with claims reduction, but present it as modeled and directional
- keep underwriting support visible as a pragmatic adjacent path
- make trust and data boundaries explicit
- ensure the product creates action, not just insight
- use AI as a strategic operating layer, not a gimmick
