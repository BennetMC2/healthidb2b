# HealthID: First-Principles Product Reset

## Purpose

This document is a zero-based reset.

It is not:

- a redesign brief
- an iteration plan for the current UI
- a defense of the current product

It is:

- a first-principles definition of what the insurer product should be if the goal is to create something buyers feel they must have

## The core question

If HealthID did not exist today, what insurer product should be built from scratch so that a buyer sees it and thinks:

`This changes how we acquire, understand, intervene on, and retain risk.`

## What the market is telling us

Across current insurance research, the pattern is consistent:

- life insurers are struggling to stay relevant and distinctive
- customer experience is weak in onboarding, servicing, and claims
- legacy technology blocks real operating change
- digital engagement is no longer optional
- AI is becoming meaningful where workflows are complex and fragmented
- claims operations, underwriting, and customer journeys all need modernization

That does not automatically validate the current product.
It does validate the category opportunity.

## Product from first principles

### What problem are we actually solving?

Not:

- “wellness”
- “wearables”
- “zero-knowledge proofs”
- “campaigns”

Those are ingredients, not the problem.

The problem is:

- life insurers have almost no high-frequency operating layer between policy issuance and claim event
- they are blind to change in customer health and behavior until very late
- they have limited trusted ways to intervene early without becoming raw-data custodians
- they need better ways to turn prevention, engagement, underwriting, and claims management into one system rather than disconnected programs

### What should the product fundamentally be?

The product should be:

- `an insurer operating system for verified health interventions`

That system has one core job:

- help an insurer define an outcome it wants to drive, identify the right lives, orchestrate the intervention, verify whether the target happened, and translate that into a business decision

### What is the irreducible workflow?

The smallest workflow that matters is:

1. insurer defines an objective
2. system identifies the reachable cohort
3. member consent and source connectivity are established
4. intervention or monitoring period runs
5. binary or structured verification outcome is produced
6. insurer sees what happened and what action should follow

If the product cannot make that loop obvious and credible, the rest is decoration.

## The buyer

### Primary economic buyer

Most likely:

- business unit leader with accountability for profitability, claims, or growth
- supported by COO / digital / transformation leadership
- gated by CIO, security, and risk

### Primary internal champion

Most likely:

- innovation or digital business lead
- claims transformation lead
- underwriting modernization lead
- customer / propositions leader

### Why they buy

They do not buy because “verified health data” sounds elegant.

They buy if they believe the product can do one or more of the following materially better than their current stack:

- reduce avoidable claims cost
- make intervention programs operational instead of aspirational
- improve selection and pre-policy screening
- create a defensible customer experience advantage
- reduce manual burden and raw-data liability

## The right product hierarchy

The current platform tends to treat campaigns as the centerpiece.

That is close, but not quite right.

The true hierarchy should be:

1. `Outcome`
2. `Cohort`
3. `Intervention`
4. `Verification`
5. `Decision`

Campaign is the container around that flow, not the strategy itself.

## The right strategic posture

### Primary story

- `claims reduction`

This is the biggest strategic prize and the strongest reason the product matters.

### Secondary story

- `underwriting / pre-policy verification`

This is the faster-to-understand adjacent use case that makes the platform feel commercially legible even when a buyer is not ready to believe the full claims-reduction case.

### Included but not dominant

- engagement
- lead generation
- renewal support

These matter, but they should not become the center of gravity.

## What must be true for the product to feel undeniable

### 1. The product must feel like an operating system, not a dashboard

Buyers do not need more views.
They need a system that appears capable of changing how work gets done.

### 2. The claims-reduction story must be real enough to survive skepticism

That means:

- the product cannot imply certainty
- it must show model assumptions cleanly
- it must explain what is verified vs inferred
- it must separate intervention logic from financial projection

### 3. The underwriting-adjacent path must feel obviously usable

If claims reduction is the visionary story, underwriting support is the proof that the engine is practical.

### 4. The trust boundary must be explicit

If a buyer cannot quickly understand:

- what data moves
- what data does not move
- what is visible
- what is simulated
- what would become real in pilot

then trust will collapse under diligence.

### 5. The system must feel like it creates leverage, not work

If the insurer team sees more complexity than leverage, they will not care.

## What the current product gets wrong from first principles

### It over-centers the interface

The product currently risks feeling like:

- a sophisticated enterprise frontend

rather than:

- a new insurer operating capability

### It still treats too much explanatory language as product value

Buyers do not value copy blocks.
They value control, evidence, and leverage.

### It does not yet separate truths cleanly enough

The system still needs a sharper distinction between:

- what is synthetic
- what is modeled
- what is target-state
- what is operationally real

### It is still too page-first

The future product should be structured around jobs and loops, not around standard SaaS navigation.

## Zero-based product definition

If rebuilding from scratch, the first version should contain only these product areas:

### 1. Strategy

Where the insurer defines:

- target business objective
- book or segment
- target cohort
- intervention logic
- decision rules

### 2. Reachable Population

Where the insurer sees:

- eligible and reachable lives
- source quality
- verification readiness
- cohort composition
- likely participation and intervention fit

### 3. Programme Execution

Where the insurer sees:

- live interventions
- verified outcomes
- drop-off points
- member progress
- operating issues

### 4. Decision Layer

Where the insurer sees:

- what happened
- what it means
- what action to take next
- which outcomes were verified vs inferred

### 5. Trust Layer

Where the insurer sees:

- receipt trail
- data boundary
- auditability
- simulation markers
- deployment and control posture

### 6. Agent Layer

Where the insurer gets:

- campaign recommendations
- claims-reduction hypotheses
- underwriting-adjacent options
- launch risks
- buyer-ready summaries

## What to keep, kill, invent

### Keep

- the campaign concept
- the cohort targeting concept
- the verification trail concept
- the idea of a right-side or on-demand strategic assistant
- the executive-grade analytical treatment

### Kill

- generic overview/dashboard behavior
- decorative trust theater
- any surface that exists only to explain the story rather than operate the workflow
- broad navigation not mapped to insurer jobs
- any number or ROI surface that feels pseudo-actuarial

### Invent

- an outcome-first insurer workspace
- a claims-reduction operating layer
- a decision layer that ties intervention to insurer action
- a precise system-boundary surface
- a real strategy agent with structured tools

## The test for greatness

The product is great when a buyer believes:

- it creates a new insurer capability
- it can fit into a real operating model
- it creates leverage across claims, underwriting, and engagement
- it respects regulatory and trust constraints
- it is not just another wellness app wearing enterprise clothes

If those are not true, the product is not yet undeniable.

## Research basis

This reset is informed by current industry signals:

- McKinsey: life insurers need new relevance, new value, and better digital/customer models
- Capgemini: onboarding, servicing, and claims remain weak, and only a small minority of insurers are best-in-class
- Deloitte: digitally enabled engagement is now core, hybrid digital-plus-human models matter, and GenAI is becoming operational across insurance workflows
- Swiss Re: dynamic underwriting, lifestyle factors, prevention, and claims transformation are becoming meaningful insurer priorities
- RGA: wellness and prevention remain promising, but measuring claims impact is difficult and discipline matters
- LIMRA: customers expect simpler, faster, clearer journeys and more value through the lifecycle
